Lightning strikes vs. Density of atmospheric discharges

Victor Batista January 4, 2025

Lightning strikes are the phenomenon over which we have the least control in engineering projects. Their incidence and frequency vary considerably around the world. In countries such as Brazil, close to the equator, their occurrence is up to ten times greater than in mid-latitudes and also ten times more frequent over continents than over oceans.

Traditionally, the keraunic index is used to assess the amount of lightning in a location, measuring the number of days with storms in the year, Td, the number of days with thunderstorms. However, the keraunic index has limitations, as it does not reflect the amount of cloud-to-ground lightning, the most relevant parameter for sizing lightning protection systems.

Currently, there are more accurate methods for characterizing lightning strikes. These methods use advanced systems that, in addition to counting the lightning that hits the ground, identify other important parameters, such as the time and location of the impact, the intensity and polarity of the main discharge, the number and intensity of subsequent discharges, and the waveforms. These systems employ a wide range of technologies, from simple spark gaps and magnetic recorders installed on tall structures to sophisticated equipment for monitoring electric and magnetic fields.

Annex F of the NBR-5419 standard provides maps of the Density of Atmospheric Discharges (Ng) for Brazil and its regions. These maps were prepared based on records of light pulses captured by the Lightning Imaging Sensor (LIS) on board NASA's Tropical Rainfall Measuring Mission (TRMM) satellite, during the period from 1998 to 2009. This same map is made available by the ONS for studies of basic design of lines and substations.
